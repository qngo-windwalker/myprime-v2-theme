    • How does alcohol affect neurotransmitter systems?

      • Alcohol affects many neurotransmitter systems, and among them are our two most active ones—the NMDA glutamate receptors (glutamate is the brain’s major natural stimulant) and the GABA receptors (GABA is the brain’s major natural sedative). These two balance each other to keep us from being too stimulated or too sedated to function.

      Alcohol in GABA receptors makes them function longer (letting more chorine ions into the cells and reducing their electrical activity), which slows the brain even more than GABA itself. At the same time, it inhibits the NMDA receptors, reducing the brain’s ability to use glutamate to stimulate itself. Both of these effects have the capacity to slow down virtually all brain functions. Since mental functions are more sensitive to small changes than are physical functions, the mental functions are impaired first and adapt, i.e. develop tolerance, more slowly than the physical functions.

      In effect, alcohol slows the brain via these mechanisms. As with everything having to do with the brain, it is more complex than this in reality and involves several other brain systems.
